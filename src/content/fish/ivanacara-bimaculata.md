commonName: Ivanacara bimaculata
scientificName: Ivanacara bimaculata
origin: Guyana, the Potaro river and the Essequibo river basins.
adultSize: Females 2.5 - 3 inches,  Males 3.5 - 4 inches
tankSize: 30 gallons or larger
foodRecommendation: Frozen adult brine shrimp, Aquarium Co op community floating
  pellets, and Black worms.
difficulty: Intermediate to Advanced
temperament: Peaceful, Can be very aggressive when babies are free
  swimming.  This species takes its job as a parent very seriously.
plantSafe: true
invertebrateSafe: false
adultMaleAppearance: Males are much longer then the female.  Their coloration is
  similar.  When free swimming males do not play the primary role in safe
  guarding young.
adultFemaleAppearance: Females are very similar in coloration to males.   The
  main difference is that their oviposter (breeding tube) descends and stay
  descended when mature.   They are also significantly smaller then the
  male.  The females are also the primary caretaker of the  brood.
husbandryNotes: >-
  I originally purchased my pair from a store on the East coast and when they
  came to me  at one inch, they weren't packed well at all.  Both the female and
  the male were suffering from ammonia burn.  The female was by far in the worst
  shape and was unable to even keep herself up off of the ground.   Over the
  next three days the male started to recover but the female took four days to
  get to the point where she could actually hold herself up.  If you're
  interested in how I handle ammonia burn in fish please consult the resource
  sheet.   I state this because I want to illustrate something.  Despite this,
  they both in fact recovered.  These are very hardy fish but they have definite
  requirements.  First and foremost they are not tolerant of ammonia.  I would
  not recommend this fish if your considering keeping them in new tanks.  These
  fish require established tanks and because of how new they are in the hobby,
  these fish have definite water requirements in order to stay healthy, breed,
  and thrive.
       As far as water requirements are concerned these fish should be kept in 30 gallon long tanks or larger.   They require a highly acidic, and soft water aquarium.   I would recommend a pH of  5.5 -  6.0 with a hardness as close to 0 as the aquarist can manage.   The fish can tolerate higher parameters but the viability of eggs goes way down if the parameters are not adequately met.   My pair did not produce viable eggs for two years after reaching adulthood.  They were conditioned using aquarium co op community pellet, spiralina enriched adult brine shrimp, hikari blood worms, and blackworms.  They did breed every few months but they never produced viable offspring, until suddenly they did.  Was it because they needed time to figure out how to reproduce or was it because of the circumstances that they came to me in.  The truth is I don't have a really good answer to this.   
       The young are large enough to take newly hatched baby brine shrimp when they  become free swimming.  As is the case with most young fish, they also heavily benefit from heavily planted tanks.  These tanks provide the environment for aufwuchs to thrive and heavily planted tanks provide a smorgasboard opportunity for when the hobbyist isn't directly providing brine shrimp.  
       Ivanacara bimaculata were model citizens, they did not destroy plants although they did frequently dig out depressions to house fry.   They were kept in tanks with neon tetras as dither fish.  They did not predate on the much smaller neon tetras and showed no aggression toward them until the babies became free swimming.  From that point the female became very aggressive, frequently chasing and lunging at the tetras she deemed too close to the fry.   The male did not take a direct hand in the raising of fry, showed no elevated aggression and seemed to be disinterested in raising the fry he sired.
