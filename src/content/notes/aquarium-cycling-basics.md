Cycling a new tank gives the filter bacteria time to grow so the aquarium can handle waste more reliably.  There are many different methods to cycle a filter.  These methods each have their strengths and each have their weaknesses.  Though the method changes, the end result is always the same, the goal is to process the waste nearly instantaneously so that any waste produced can be converted to nitrite and nitrate.  Here are some of the methods that have been traditionally used.



1.)  Using Chemical or Existing Biological Media:

-  The cheat method of cycling tank starts as all tanks do by setting up the tank as normal.  The filter is selected.  Usually this filter is either a sponge or a HoB (hang on the back).  Then you use a product such as Fritz Zyme 7, ADA NATURE AQURIUM BACTER 100, or API Quickstart and you dose the tank as prescribed by the manufacturer into the aquarium.   After 24 hours and once per day a small water change is done until ammonia reaches zero.  The ammonia can be fed directly into the tank, through a sacrificial fish, or by taking a chunk of shrimp or similar meat and allowing it to breakdown into the tank.
- One variation on this method is to instead of purchasing the bacteria from a manufacturer, you take an already existing sponge filter or cannister and you squeeze it out into the new tank.  You then wait 24 to 48 hours and begin cycling the aquarium using the bacteria from an already seeded filter.  It should be noted that this is a a very quick way of cycling new filters.  Like before either a sacrificial fish is introduced, or a chunk of meat can be added allowing it to breakdown in the tank and create ammonia for the bacteria to metabolize.  

2.)  Slow and steady:

-  The slow and steady method of cycling tank starts as all tanks do by setting up the tank as normal.  The filter is selected and started.  Then you add either fishfood, organic waste, or ammonia and then you wait.  You will then wait and you will wait and you will wait.  Weeks later, the filter should be established enough to process waste and to safely support fish.   The sign that the aquarium is ready occurs when bacterial blooms end.   A bacterial bloom looks like a white or light brown haze in the tank that eventually clears up.  When it does your filter has processed the excess nutrient load.  A water change is commonly done and then fish are added.
